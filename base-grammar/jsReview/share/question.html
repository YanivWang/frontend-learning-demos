Part I

1. "저는...입니다 / jeoneun... ibnida"  

Korean Sentences Pronunciation English Translation

저는 중국 사람입니다.

jeoneun jung-gug salam-ibnida.

I am Chinese.

저는 한국 사람입니다.

jeoneun hangug salam-ibnida.

I am Korean.

저는 미국 사람입니다.

jeoneun migug salam-ibnida.

I am American.

 

"저" (jeo) is the first-person pronoun as "I" in English. 

"은/는" (eun/neun) are the Korean topic-marking particles. 

 

① The particles "은/는" are used for the following purposes:

  • To introduce a topic or a subject
  • To show emphasis
  • To show contrast

 

② "은/는" are placed after:

  • Nouns
  • Pronouns
  • Numbers

 

③ How do you choose between "은/는"?

  • When the noun ends with a vowel, attach "는" to the noun.
  • When the noun ends with a consonant, attach "은" to the noun.


For example: 

Example Explanation

salam-eun

사람은

people

The noun "사람" ends with the consonant "ㅁ", attach "은".

jeoneun

저는

I am

The pronoun "저" ends with the vowel "ㅓ", attach "는".

 

3) "입니다" (ibnida) is one of the conjugated forms of the verb "이다" (ida) (to be). Here it serves as one of the endings for declarative sentences. More detailed rules and explanations on Korean verbs will be unfolded as we delve deeper into the Korean language.

 

2. Polite Form: ㅂ니다 / 습니다 (bnida/seubnida)

In Korea, the speech level Polite Form is required on many occasions. The politeness of a sentence is usually indicated by the suffix of the sentence-ending verb. In the current stage of LingoDeer's Korean course, we will learn two forms of polite suffixes: ㅂ니다/습니다 (bnida/seubnida) and 아요/어요/여요 (ayo/eoyo/yeoyo).

In LingoDeer's Korean course, we will learn the Formal Polite Suffixes: ㅂ니다/습니다 (bnida/seubnida) first. You can hear this type of sentence endings on many formal occasions, such as in news programs, company meetings, or in the military. 

The conjugation of the Formal Polite suffix is relatively easy, which is great for beginners to get familiar with the infinitive form of verbs and adjectives in Korean.

After the Formal Polite Form, we will learn the Informal Polite Suffixes: 아요/어요/여요 (ayo/eoyo/yeoyo) later in the course. It is more commonly used by Koreans in daily conversations. However, to master the Informal Polite suffixes, a solid grasp of the infinitive form of Korean verbs and adjectives is required. Therefore, this course has been set up to teach the Formal Polite suffixes first.

 

3. Korean Sentence Structure

In the sentence "저는 한국 사람입니다/jeoneun hangug salam-ibnida/I'm Korean", it's easy to notice that "입니다" (ibnida/to be) is placed at the end of the sentence. 

In Korean sentences, the verb is ALWAYS at the end of the sentence. 

Unlike the structure of English sentences, Korean uses a Subject-Object-Verb order to build sentences.

For example: "I learn Korean"

2. Spacing

Spacing is mandatory in written Korean because, just like English, it would be hard, though not impossible, to understand a sentence without spaces. Spaces are used to separate words from each other, and a word or 단어 in Korean is a character combination that can be used on its own. But there is no need to put spaces between particles and the words that precede them. Sentence endings also follow the words that precede them directly without spaces.

For example, in the sentence "저는 중국 사람입니다",  the independent words are "저", "중국", 사람". Since "는" is a particle and "입니다" a sentence ending, there are no spaces before them.
